All Unemployed individuals who are Legally Eligible to work in British Columbia can access Employment Program of BC ("EPBC" or "Program") services.  Some services offered through the Program have specific eligibility requirements.

In February 2009, the Canada-BC Labour Market Development Agreement (LMDA) came into effect.  The LMDA transferred the responsibility from Canada to BC for programs and services aimed at helping Employment Insurance (EI) Clients to prepare for, find and keep work.

LMDAs between the federal and provincial or territorial governments in Canada are authorized under the Employment Insurance (EI) Act.  Funding for LMDAs is provided through the Employment Insurance Account, which is paid for solely through employer and employee EI contributions.  Under the LMDA, BC operates employment programs similar to the Employment Benefits and Support Measures authorized under Part II of the EI Act.

EI Clients, as defined in the Program Glossary, can access EPBC services, including Self-Serve and Case Managed Services.

POLICY SECTION EI Client Participation

The LMDA and the Employment Program of BC

With the LMDA, BC assumed the responsibility for the design, delivery and management of programming similar to the Employment Benefits and Supports Measures (EBSM) under Part II of the EI Act.

When revisions are considered to the standard EBSMs, the Province of BC must submit the proposed changes to the federal governments Department of Human Resources and Skills Development Canada (HRSDC).  HRSDC undertakes a process referred to as the "test of similarity" to ensure that programs in all jurisdictions across Canada are similar to the federal EBSMs.

 

The EPBC integrates LMDA funded federal programming with income assistance employment programs funded by the Province of BC.  The Program provides employment services to assist British Columbians to prepare for, find and keep Employment, and has gone through the federal "test of similarity" process to determine that it is aligned with the key characteristics of the EBSMs.  This is essential for LMDA funds to be used for EPBC programming.

 

The LMDA also authorized designated officials in BC to make referrals under Section 25 of the EI Act, which allows Active EI Claimants to collect EI benefits while participating in EPBC services that normally require full-time participation (Training, Self-Employment Services, and Job Creation Partnerships).

Employment Insurance (EI) Act

Canadas EI Act provides temporary financial assistance for Unemployed workers while they look for work.  Canadians who are sick, pregnant or caring for a newborn or adopted child, as well as those who must care for a family member who is seriously ill with a significant risk of death, may also receive temporary Employment Insurance benefits.  Funding under the EI Act is divided into:

  • Part I of the EI Act provides temporary income support (EI benefits) for Unemployed individuals who meet the eligibility criteria defined by the Act; and
  • Part II of the EI Act provides for the Employment Benefits and Support Measures which are employment programs and services designed to help Unemployed people return to work.  Under the EBSMs:
  • Employment Benefits provide funding to assist Unemployed job seekers who are currently receiving regular EI Part I benefits, or who have been eligible for EI Part I benefits, in the past 36 months, or who have been on parental or maternity benefits in the previous 60 months to gain needed skills or work experience for the labour market or to become self-employed.  These Legacy Programs are known as Skills Development, Self-Employment, Job Creation Partnerships and Targeted Wage Subsidies. In the EPBC, the services that provide employment benefits include Training, Self-Employment (SE), Job Creation Partnerships (JCP) and Wage Subsidy (WS) Work Experience Placements.
  • Support Measures are used to fund organizations, employers, communities and associations to assist unemployed persons; to develop and implement strategies for dealing with labour force adjustments, meeting human resource planning requirements; and to do research and development projects aimed at finding better ways to assist people find, and keep, employment.  These Legacy Programs include Employment Assistance Services, Labour Market Partnerships and Research and Innovation. In the EPBC the Support Measures include Self-Serve Services, Case Management and most Case Managed Services are funded as support measures.  Additionally, the Community and Employer Partnerships programs (Job Creation Partnerships Projects, Labour Market Partnerships Projects, and Research and Innovation projects) of the EPBC, which are funded through the Partnership and Innovation (P & I) Fund are considered Support Measures.

Eligibility

To be eligible for Program Financial Supports as an EI Client, the Client must meet the individual service eligibility criteria and be an EI Client as defined under the LMDA.  Refer to the Glossary of Program Terms for the detailed definition of an EI Client.

If a Client is on an active EI claim they must have a referral under Section 25 of the EI Act to be able to collect EI benefits while participating in the employment services of Training, Self-Employment and Job Creation Partnerships.  As part of the LMDA, Canada has delegated the authority to make referrals under Section 25 of the EI Act to designated officials in BC.

 

Determining Employment Insurance (EI) Eligibility
 
It is Service Canada that determines an individuals EI eligibility and administers the EI Act.  The Province of BC can view a Clients EI Status through a web-based application called LMDA Access, which Service Canada has made available to LMDA holders throughout Canada.
 
LMDA Access is provided to designated staff of the Ministrys Employment and Labour Market Services Division (ELMSD).  Ministry funded EPBC Service Providers do not have direct access, but EI Clients can access information about their EI eligibility status and EPBC Service Providers can request needed information from the Ministry when required to confirm an individuals eligibility for services as outlined below
 
Initial EI Eligibility Checks
 
An EI eligibility check is not required for Clients who are only requesting Self-Serve Services or for a Client to be accepted into Case Management. Determining whether or not a Client is an EI Client is important in relation to Client eligibility for specific services and whether services and supports should be funded from the LMDA or Provincial component of the EPBC Contract budget. This is normally done at the time a Client is identified as needing a Formal Needs Assessment (FNA) and before preparing to develop an Action Plan, to support the Case Manager in developing the Action Plan/Service Plan with the Client.  EPBC Service Providers must have Clients seeking EPBC Case Management undertake an initial EI eligibility check when Clients who are or will be Case Managed indicate that they:
 
         have worked or established an EI claim of any type in the past 3 years; or
         have received benefits through a maternity or parental EI claim in the past 5 years; or
         are uncertain if they have worked or established an EI claim in this time period. 
 
EPBC Service Providers are to request information on an individuals EI Status directly from the Client, when a Client identifies that he or she is, or may be, an EI Client, either as an Active EI Claimant or a Reachback Client.  The Client/EPBC Service Provider performs the initial eligibility check through either the Clients online My Service Canada Account or by calling Service Canada at 1-800-206-7218. 
 
Every EI Client has access to their individual information through web access to My Service Canada Account (MSCA)or by telephone or by requesting information in-person from Service Canada or through other official documentation (e.g. a copy of an EI cheque stub, or other official record from MCSA or Revenue Canada statement).  MSCA provides a single point of access to view and update information with Employment Insurance (EI), Canada Pension Plan (CPP) and Old Age Security (OAS).  Once logged on to the My Service Canada Account, Clients are able to view and update their EI information.
 
If calling Service Canada, to ensure the Clients personal information is protected, it must be the Client who makes the call and it must be the Client that allows the EPBC Case Manager to hear the response regarding their EI Client status eligibility.
 
Ministry EI Eligibility Checks
 
EPBC Service Providers can request an EI eligibility check from the Ministry (ELMSD) only under specific circumstances:
 
When Required by EPBC Policy: 
 
         While most services of the Program can be delivered through the LMDA component of the EPBC Contract budget to all Clients, some EPBC services and Financial Supports require that  EI eligibility be reconfirmed with the Ministry for EI Clients.  These include: Training; Wage Subsidy Work Experience Placements; Job Creation Partnerships Placements; and, Self-Employment Services.   A Ministry EI eligibility check is to be used to reconfirm a Clients EI eligibility for these specific services after:
 
o    the initial EI eligibility check has confirmed that a Client is an EI Client;
o    the EPBC Service Provider has assessed the Client's needs and confirmed that the Client needs an LMDA funded service for which a Ministry EI eligibility check is required; and
o    the Client has completed and signed a formal application to participate in a service that requires a Ministry EI eligibility check.  
 
The Ministry eligibility check will also confirm benefit rate and duration for Active EI Claimants, prior to the EPBC Service Provider determining/negotiating an appropriate level of Program Financial Supports and entering into a Financial Agreement with the Client.
 
When a Clients EI eligibility cannot be confirmed through an Initial EI eligibility check and confirmation of EI Status is essential for provision of services:
 
         In very exceptional circumstances, when a Client's EI eligibility status cannot be confirmed through Service Canada verification or documentation for an initial EI eligibility check, and there is risk, based on information provided by the Client, that EI Reachback status may have ended, a Ministry EI eligibility check may be requested.  A Ministry EI eligibility check should only be undertaken if an EPBC Service Provider cannot determine potential EI eligibility for a needed service that requires it and a rationale must be documented in the Client's EPBC Case File rationalizing why the Ministry EI eligibility check is required at this stage of service. 
 
Note: A Ministry EI eligibility check can only confirm the Clients EI eligibility status as of the date the eligibility check is requested.  In these situations, the Ministry (ELMSD) is unable to determine or confirm how long a Reachback Clients EI Status will continue to be in effect.  If the Client is assessed as needing a service that requires a Ministry EI eligibility check, as is normally the case with all EI Clients, another Service Request to reconfirm EI eligibility at the time the Client completes an application for the service is required to verify if client is still eligible as an EI Client.
 
Ministry EI eligibility checks will only be processed when a signed Client application for a service that requires an EI eligibility check is recorded on the EPBC Client Case file or in exceptional cases as explained above, a rationale is provided as to why a Ministry EI eligibility check is being requested. EPBC Service Providers must also provide a comment in the Service Request MEMO field to refer the Ministry (CU) to the Case File Notes where the rationale for such requests is to be documented.

 

 

EI Part I as First Payer

It is Ministry Policy that a claimant who is entitled to EI Part I benefits and has been approved to participate in an EPBC service be paid EI Part I benefits prior to or in conjunction with Program Financial Supports.

EI Reporting

EI Clients on active EI Part I claims are required to complete reports every two weeks (EI REPORT CARDS) to maintain their eligibility for benefits.  EI reports can be completed on-line, by telephone or as paper report cards.  Most Clients complete their reports on-line through their My Service Canada Account.  When an Active EI Part I Claimant is assisted through one of the EPBC services, they will normally have a Section 25 Referral on their EI claim and will have to respond differently to the questions asked by EI. 

Active EI Part I Claimants who are approved for Self-Employment (SE) Services will automatically receive paper reporting cards.

EI Claim Status

When viewing LMDA Access, Ministry (ELMSD) staff will see four different EI claim status:  ActiveRegular Benefits; ActiveSpecial Benefits; Eligible; and Not Eligible.

EI Claim Status Claim Type Eligible for EI funded Services
Active Regular Benefits (including Found Work, Disqualified, and Disentitled) Yes
Active Special Benefits No
Eligible Meets EI Client definition for previous claim Yes
Not Eligible Does not meet EI Client definition for previous claim No

Definitions of EI Claim Status in LMDA Access

Active Claim Regular Benefits

Individuals who have established a claim and are in receipt of Regular Benefits are identified as priority Clients under the LMDA for employment services.

Active ClaimFound Work is a status used by Service Canada to describe a claimant who found work or returned to work before the end of their claim.

When an individuals EI claim status is verified in LMDA Access as Active ClaimFound Work the Client should be advised to contact Service Canada to re-establish the claim.

Active Claim Disqualified or Active Claim-Disentitled

Disqualifications and/or Disentitlements are a denial of EI benefits for definite or indefinite number of weeks.  They are imposed by Service Canada for a variety of reasons, usually when the Client is not in compliance with federal legislation or regulations.  A Disqualification or Disentitlement means that although the Client has established an EI claim, he or she will not be able to collect EI Part I benefits for a temporary or indefinite period.  The time period of the Disentitlement/Disqualification will be displayed on LMDA Access.

Some Disqualifications/Disentitlements can be suspended when a Client is referred to an employment service.  The possible messages on LMDA Access are:

  • Disqualification/Disentitlement suspended for Part II:  This message means that the disqualification or disentitlement will be automatically suspended when a Section 25 Referral is input on the claim.
  • Disqualification/Disentitlement cannot be suspended. Claimant not eligible for Part I:  This message means that the disqualification or disentitlement cannot be suspended.

If a Client has a disentitlement or disqualification on their EI Claim, the Client is still eligible for services under the EPBC as an EI Client.  If a Client has been disentitled to EI Part I benefits, the reason for this should be discussed with the Client as it may impact the Clients ability to undertake the necessary steps to return to work.

Active Claim Special Benefits Claims (Maternity, Parental, Sickness and Compassionate Care)

Individuals receiving EI Part I Special Benefits are not normally to be considered for employment services or Program Financial Supports, as Special Benefits are paid to individuals based upon specific criteria and they may have employment to return to.  If a Client on Active ClaimSpecial Benefits requests service, they must be able to show that they have no job to return to and the Client would need to change their EI claim to a Regular Claim.

Eligible

Former EI claimants, who are also commonly referred to as Reachback, are eligible as EI Clients under the LMDA if:

  • They had an EI claim (any type) that was established within the past three years; or
  • They received maternity or parental benefits in the last five years and are now returning to the labour market.

LMDA Access has been programmed to determine this status based on the following factors:

  • An  EI Claim 36 months from the end date of the EI claim; or
  • For a Maternity or Parental benefits claim 60 months from the start date of the Special Benefits EI Claim to the date the request for assistance is made.

Service Canada has advised that there is no expectation for provinces to validate EI Client status for Reachback Clients beyond the information provided in LMDA Access.  LMDA Access is considered the official determinate of EI Client status for the purpose of confirming eligibility related to provision of services available only to EI Clients under the terms of the LMDA.

 

Eligible EI Pending or Last ROE Missing

If a Client has applied for EI benefits in the previous four to six weeks, Service Canada will indicate this on LMDA Access as EI Pending or Last ROE missing.  It is important to note that if a Client has had a previous claim, they may also meet the definition of a Reachback Client in terms of meeting Program eligibility as an EI Client.

An EI Claim status of Eligible EI Pending or EligibleLast ROE missing means that the Client is currently eligible for EPBC services and Program Financial Supports, but Service Canada has not finished its assessment for a new claim.   The Ministry can confirm EI Client status as Eligible through LMDA Access, but cannot confirm if the Client has an active EI claim for the purpose of determining need for Financial Supports until Service Canada has completed its assessment.

Clients

A Client is not considered an EI Client under the LMDA if:

  • They have not established an EI claim within the past 3 years; or
  • They have not received maternity or parental benefits in the last 5 years.

Ineligible EI Pending or Last ROE Missing

If a Client has applied for EI benefits in the previous four to six weeks, Service Canada will indicate this on LMDA Access as EI Pending or Last ROE missing.  However, as the Client has not had an EI claim within the Reachback period, they are currently not eligible as an EI Client under the EPBC.

As the Client has an application for EI benefits with Service Canada, this status may change if the Client can establish an EI claim.  It is primarily the Clients responsibility to maintain contact with Service Canada and notify their EPBC Case Manager if EI claim status changes.

Other Claim Types Fishing and Manual Pay System (MPS)

Fishing claims are a specific type of EI claim that is only available to people that are self-employed in fishing.  Unlike regular EI, which is based on hours of work, EI fishing benefits are based upon earnings.

Fishing claims do not have the same duration rules as regular EI claims.  For fishing claims, the maximum entitlement is 26 weeks and the duration of a claim is set by whether the claim is for Summer Fishing Benefits or for Winter Fishing Benefits.  Summer Fishing Benefits may begin no earlier than the week in which October 1st falls and must end in the week that June 15th falls.  Winter Fishing Benefits may begin no earlier than the week in which April 1st falls and must end the week that December 15th falls.  Fishing benefits are considered as Regular Benefits, but they show as a MPS (Manual Pay System) claim in LMDA Access.

Manual Pay System

Manual Pay System (MPS): Some EI claims can not be entered into Service Canadas On-line Insurance System (OLIS) and need to be dealt with through their Manual Pay System. In LMDA Access, these claims will show as StatusMPS ClaimContact Service Canada.

For MPS Claims, information needs to be verified through Service Canada EI eligibility checks, and Section 25 data would be sent to Service Canada.

EI and the Family Supplement

The Family Supplement is a feature of EI that provides additional benefits to low-income families with children. The EI Client does not have to apply for it; if they are eligible for it, it will automatically be added to the EI benefit payment.

How is the Family Supplement determined?

If the EI claimant or their spouse receives the Canada Child Tax Benefit (CCTB), a program administered by Canada Revenue Agency (CRA), then they are eligible to receive the EI Family Supplement on their family net income up to and including $25,921 per year.

The Family Supplement rate is based on:

  • Family net income up to a maximum of $25,921 per year; and
  • The number of children in the family and their ages.

The maximum Family Supplement can be as high as 80% of an EI claimants average insurable earnings. If both parents apply for EI at the same time, only one parent can receive the Family Supplement. Usually, it is better for the spouse with the lower benefit rate to receive the Family Supplement.

As family income level rises, the Family Supplement gradually decreases, so that when the maximum income of $25,921 is reached, no supplement is payable. Further information is available on the EI website.

Following are examples of the supplement paid based on family net income:

  • Family net income is below $20,921 the full Family Supplement would be paid.
    • If the family net income is $20,000, with 3 children aged 6, 8 and 10, the EI claimant could receive $86.10 per week plus $4.15 extra supplement because one child is younger than 7 years old.
  • Family net income is between $20,921 and $25,921 a partial Family Supplement would be paid.
    • If the family net income is $22,000 with three children aged 6, 8 and 10, the EI claimant could receive $68.20 per week plus $3.45 extra supplement because of the child under 7 years old.

In all cases, no Family Supplement will be paid beyond the current maximum EI weekly rate.

LMDA Access and the Family Supplement

Although, the Family Supplement is paid by Service Canada through the EI system, the value of the Family Supplement does not appear on LMDA Access due to the Federal Privacy Legislation. This means that for low income earners, the amount that they receive from Service Canada may be different than the amount that is displayed on LMDA Access.

Section 25 Authorizations and Referrals under Part I of the EI Act

Individuals who are receiving EI Part I Regular Benefits are required to be capable of, available for and seeking work. Individuals (including Apprentices) referred to training, SE Services or JCP require a Section 25 Authorization and Referral to enable them to continue receiving EI Part I benefits during their benefit period while participating in one of these approved EPBC services.

The Section 25 Referral excuses Active EI Claimants from being available for and seeking work when they would normally be disentitled for not being available for employment.

Under the LMDA, Canada delegated Section 25 authority to designated Ministry (ELMSD) officials and a Section 25 Referral can only be made by an authorized official to which authority has been delegated.

Section 25 of the EI Act states:

S.25 (1) For the purposes of this Part, a claimant is Unemployed and capable of and available for work during a period when the claimant is

(a) Attending a course or program of instruction or training at the claimants own expense, or under employment benefits or similar benefits that are the subject of an agreement under Section 63, to which the Commission, or an authority that the Commission designates, has referred the claimant; or

(b) Participating in any other employment activity

(i) for which assistance has been provided for the claimant under prescribed employment benefits or benefits that are the subject of an agreement under Section 63 and are similar to the prescribed employment benefits, and

(ii) to which the Commission, or an authority that the Commission designates, has referred the Claimant.

(2) A decision of the Commission about the referral of a claimant to a course, program or other employment activity mentioned in subsection (1) is not subject to appeal under Section 114 or 115 of the EI Act.

When is a Section 25 Authorization and Referral needed?

A Section 25 Referral occurs when a Section 25 Referral authority:

  • Assesses a Client to determine needs;
  • Determines what programs/services they are able to direct the Client to in order to address their needs; and
  • Determines whether or not these programs/services fall within the terms and conditions of the program delivered through the LMDA.

The entry of the referral through LMDA Access will inform Service Canada that the Client has been referred and that the Client should continue to be allowed to receive EI Part I benefits while participating in the service.

What Legislation delegates authority to the Provinces with regard to Section 25 of the EI Act?

The Employment Insurance Commission, pursuant to subsection 31(3) of Canadas Department of Human Resources and Skills Development Act, authorizes the Organization to exercise the Commissions power to designate authorities that may, for the purposes of Section 25 of the EI Act, refer Active EI Claimants to:

a) Courses or programs of instruction or training which the claimant is attending at his or her own expense; or

b) Any other employment activity for which assistance has been provided for the claimant under labour market programs described in Part I, Section 1 of Schedule B which are similar to the Self-Employment Employment Benefit and the Job Creation Partnerships Employment Benefit established by the Commission under the EI Act.

Does the Section 25 authority refer to part-time or full-time services?

A Section 25 authorization should be made only for services that are considered full-time, but occasionally part-time training is in the best interest of the Client. This does not mean that a Section 25 Referral is required. If the Ministry (ELMSD), as the referral authority, determines that the part-time training does not warrant approval under Section 25 of the EI Act, the EI Client can apply to Service Canada where EI officials will determine eligibility based on the claimants availability as it relates to Section 18 of the EI Act (i.e. based on whether the training does or does not interfere with the Clients availability for work).

Normally, if the Client receives any Program Financial Supports for the part-time training (e.g. tuition), a Section 25 Referral should be made as this ensures that the Client has the necessary weekly living supports to complete the training. However, it is not always necessary to have a Section 25 Referral for part-time training. Case Managers and authorized Ministry staff should consider the following when a Client is considered for a Section 25 for part-time training:

  • Can the Client work and take the training?
  • Is there a risk that the Client would not be able to complete the training without the Section 25 authorization?

If a Section 25 Referral is not made, the Client should be advised to discuss their training with Service Canada and determine how to report it on their EI claim.

What if a Client is receiving EI Special Benefits and wants to be referred to an EPBC Service (Training, Self-Employment or JCP)?

Claimants cannot be referred to EPBC services while receiving Special Benefits. A referral can only be input on a Regular Benefits claim. A claimant must qualify for and be eligible to receive Regular Benefits in order to be paid benefits while involved in an employment initiative to which they have been referred by a designated authority under Section 25 of the EI Act.

There are instances where EI claimants do attend training on their own initiative (non-referred training) while receiving Special Benefits. Since EI claimants on Special Benefits (except sickness benefits) are not required to be ready, willing and capable of work, this is permitted under the EI Act.

An Active EI Claimant receiving Special Benefits may enquire about referral to training or employment services, and through the Client assessment process it may be determined that a referral to training is appropriate for that Client. In exceptional circumstances outlined in the Authorization to Quit Policy, the EPBC Case Manager may counsel a Client to leave their employment or not return to their employment after their Special Benefits. The claimant would be given an Authorization to Quit letter, however the Client would be required to terminate their Special Benefits and establish a Regular Benefits claim to be eligible to receive EI benefits while in a referred training or employment initiative.

In such circumstances, if a claimant was receiving Sickness benefits, a medical certificate from a physician could be required to confirm that the claimant is medically capable of working or attending school.

Which EPBC Services require a Section 25 Referral?

In the EPBC, Clients who are Active EI Claimants require Section 25 Referrals for participation in Training Services (other than Short Term Orientation and Certificate Training), including Apprentices and Feepayers, SE Services and JCP Work Experience Placements.  The Ministry (ELMSD) may require additional documentation to support a Section 25 Authorization and Referral request.

Active EI Clients and participation in Case Management, Employment Counselling and Group Workshops

A Client on an active EI claim who is working with a Case Manager and is undertaking an employment needs assessment or other assessment, employment counselling or group workshops, is not eligible for a Section 25 Referral as these are services that should support and not negatively impact a Clients availability for work.

Active EI Claimants must continue to be available and seeking work to be able to continue to receive EI benefits throughout their EI benefit period, except when authorized under Section 25 to participate in the specific services described in the section above.

What does Service Canada consider acceptable proof of a Section 25 Referral for BC?

All that is necessary is that a Section 25 Referral be entered through LMDA Access by authorized Ministry (ELMSD) staff with the appropriate delegated authority and security screening.

Interruptions and Breaks in Training

Scheduled Breaks

A scheduled break refers to a break that training institutions have during programs of instruction, these are breaks that the institution publishes, and they are normally the break between semesters at Christmas, the reading break and the Summer Break. These breaks are divided into two categories, breaks of 3 weeks or less, and breaks of 4 weeks or more.

Breaks of 3 weeks or less

EI Clients that are attending training authorized under the EPBC may continue to receive financial assistance during scheduled breaks that are three weeks or less in duration. During a scheduled break of 3 weeks or less EI claimants would continue to report to EI that they are participating in their EPBC service as outlined in the How to complete your EI Cards.

The Section 25 input for EI clients on Active EI claims would include the authorized break period as outlined in the LMDA Access procedures.

Breaks of more than 3 weeks

EI Clients that are attending training authorized under the EPBC will not receive Financial Supports for living during breaks in training that are longer in duration than 3 weeks. Active EI Claimants will not have Section 25 Referrals during a break of 4 weeks or more and must be meet the EI criteria of available for work during this period to be eligible for EI Part I benefits.

It is very important that Case Manager review the training program to identify the scheduled breaks and the policy for scheduled breaks needs to be discussed with clients. The client needs to be aware that for any scheduled break of longer than 3 weeks, he or she must be available and looking for work during the entire break period.

Note: No portion of a scheduled break of longer than 3 weeks can be authorized under Section 25.

Section 25 Referrals for courses with break(s) longer than 3 weeks will need to be input as two or more Section 25 Referrals as outlined in the LMDA Access procedures. Any period of time that is not authorized is not input as part of the period covered by the Section 25 Referral.

For Example Client is approved for training starting March 5, 2012 and ending April 20, 2013, there is a scheduled 6 week break July 1 to Aug 10, 2012 and a 2 week break December 23 to January 5, 2013.

Clients EI claim start is March 4, 2012 and he is eligible for 48 weeks of benefits.

Section 25 would be entered as:

  • First Section 25 Start March 5, 2012 End June 30, 2012
  • Second Section 25 Start August 13, 2012 End April 20, 2013 and a break entered as Break Start December 23, 2012 Break End January 5, 2013
Scheduled Breaks and Out of Country Travel

EI claimants on training may travel during scheduled breaks. However, the EI Act requires that EI claimants remain in Canada while in receipt of benefits unless prescribed by EI Regulations. Therefore, an Active EI Claimant participating in a course in Canada and who leaves the country during an approved break will be disentitled to benefits for the time they are out of the country. Active EI Claimants who intend to leave the country during a break should be advised to inform Service Canada of the dates they will be out of the country.

Labour Disputes or Uncontrollable Events

EPBC Clients affected by labour disputes are not expected to cross picket lines and therefore may experience a gap in programming. A similar gap may be experienced as a result of an event such as a natural disaster. In either situation, the policy concerning continuation of Financial Supports, as well as EI benefits, during the gap in participation is the same.

Generally, in such situations Clients may continue to receive Financial Supports and EI benefits for up to three weeks, after which the Section 25 Referral would normally be suspended. The three week period would start the week following the last day of Client activity. If, as the three week point approaches, it is apparent that the interruption will end in the near future, the Section 25 may be extended with the approval of senior Ministry (ELMSD) officials (e.g. Executive Director, Assistant Deputy Minister). Clients whose intervention is suspended because of an officially recognized pandemic may have their Section 25 continued for up to six weeks.

In all cases, if the Client quits the EPBC service, the Section 25 Referral authorization would be terminated effective the date of quitting. Similarly, if the course or work is cancelled and no alternative can be found within a reasonable time period (three weeks), the Section 25 must be terminated.

In situations where a labour dispute or natural disaster results in the closure of entire classes or training institutions or work site for which there is three weeks or less of time left in the course or program, the Section 25 would continue until the established end date of the course, or until the course is cancelled, whichever is sooner.

If the closure will be more than three weeks (such as a natural disaster which will require the institution to carry out extensive repairs), Clients can continue to receive Financial Supports for up to three weeks while they await an announcement concerning the resumption, most likely at an alternative location. If the institution or work place host is not able to resume courses or work, the Clients Section 25 Referral will be suspended after the three week period, until the training begins again or the Client, together with his/her Case Manager, reassesses his/her needs and develops a revised Action Plan.

As always, judgment should be used in suspending or terminating a Section 25 Referral for individuals participating in an EPBC service. If, for example, the institution will not re-open for a year, the Section 25 agreement with the participant would not be suspended, it would be terminated. If the Client was able to find alternative training at another institution, the Section 25 Referral would then need to be amended. There is also the option of re-assessing the Clients needs and exploring other services to support a return to work, which may result in a revised Action Plan and a new Financial Agreement.

Clients who are in receipt of EI benefits must be advised that after the specified period of time (normally three weeks), they must be available and looking for work as the Section 25 Referral will cease once the EPBC Service is suspended or terminated.

It is the responsibility of the Client to advise their Case Manager of any changes to their participation or status.

The Case Manager must formally advise the Client in writing of the suspension of the Section 25 Referral, resulting in the requirement to be available for work. This can be done through a letter to the Client or an amendment to the Clients Financial Agreement. EI claimants would continue to receive their Part I benefits, on the condition that they continue to meet their obligations.

In the case of an officially recognized pandemic, Clients required to stay at home because of sickness or to care for sick family members will have their Section 25 authorization continued for a maximum of three consecutive weeks. In situations where the Client is caring for a sick family member, school work should be continued at home if at all possible.

Permanent School Closure Bankruptcy

In situations where a school closes due to bankruptcy, Financial Supports would continue to be paid to Clients for a maximum of three weeks. Efforts should be made to move the Clients to a new institution to finish their training where possible.

What Information can ELMSD share with Service Canada?

In accordance with the LMDA, in addition to the information required to enter a Section 25 Referral on an EI claim, the Ministry (ELMSD) can provide specific information to Service Canada when it is suspected that the individual was/is not entitled to EI benefits received while participating in an EPBC service authorized under Section 25 of the EI Act.

In these cases, the following information can be provided:

  • Client Name;
  • Client Address;
  • Social Insurance Number (SIN);
  • Reason Client is not capable of work/intervention/service;
  • Reason Client is not available for work/intervention/service;
  • Reason earnings not reported to Canada during the weeks they were earned;
  • Dates(s) absent from Canada/area without authorization;
  • Reason absent from Canada/area without authorization;
  • Reason for separation from employment;
  • Whether or not the Client reported for an interview as directed;
  • Interview details (counsellor seen/scheduled to be seen, date, time and location of interview);
  • Method used to direct Client to report;
  • Reasons for not reporting (e.g. no contact, not interested in service, family obligations, transportation difficulties, unable to attendand any reason given, withdrawal from the labour forceand any reason given);
  • Date of return to work;
  • Employer Name and telephone number;
  • Reason for withdrawal from program; and
  • Reason for termination from program.

Other Considerations for Section 25 Referrals

Self-Employment Clients and Out of Country Work/Travel

Under SE Services policy, in exceptional circumstances, SE Clients may be granted permission to travel outside of the country to seek expert advice and/or for other business-related reason that support the successful development and implementation of their business. For SE Clients who are Active EI Claimants, this notification must also be shared with Service Canada for documentation to the Clients EI file, to ensure that Service Canada does not disentitle the Client from receiving EI benefits. EPBC Service Providers must notify the Ministry (ELMSD Centralized Unit) of SE Clients approved to go out of the country for reasons related to their business at least two weeks in advance of the scheduled travel date. In turn, the Ministry (ELMSD) will inform Service Canada of this approval.

Self-Employment Clients and EI Report Cards

Once a Section 25 Referral to SE Services has been input on an active EI claim, Service Canada will change the Clients claim reporting method to manually-completed paper report cards. These paper report cards are mailed to the Client every two weeks by Service Canada and must be completed and returned to Service Canada in accordance with their completion instructions.

As most EI Clients complete their report cards either by telephone or through the Internet, this can result in timing issues, especially if the start date of the SE Services referral splits the reporting period. Occasionally, the Client will need to telephone Service Canada (1-800-206-7218) for instructions on how to complete the report card in these circumstances.

Other Considerations for EI Clients

Date of Application for Financial Supports

This is the date a formal signed application for Financial Supports is received by the EPBC Service Provider for an EI Client who is being considered for approval to participate in Training, SE Services, JCP or WS Work Experience Placements. The application date determines whether the Client is eligible for Program Financial Supports as an EI Client under the LMDA.

The initial determination of EI Client status must becl completed well in advance of the official confirmation as described in Determining EI Client Status above.

As the process of needs determination and information gathering during Case Management can take considerable timeUpdatedboth the Client and the Case Manager should be aware of the implications of the "Date of Application". There are two guiding principles:

  • A request for Financial Supports under an EPBC service follows (not precedes) a decision by the Client to participate in the employment service that has been identified and agreed to as needed by the Client and EPBC Service Provider; and
  • The date of this decision is documented in the EPBC Client Case File.

Reconfirming EI Benefit End Date after Service Start Date

For EPBC Clients who are, or will be, receiving Financial Supports in addition to EI Part I benefits, it is important that the Case Manager asks for a confirmation of the EI end date from the Ministry. Confirmation of the Part I benefit end date should be done within four weeks of the expected end date that was communicated to the EPBC Service Provider on the EI eligibility check at the time the Client was approved to participate in an EPBC service requiring a Section 25 Referral.

EI claims are based on a calculation of the eligible weeks to which the EI claimant is entitled. These entitlement weeks can be paid within a certain period normally 52 weeks. However, if a claimant does not receive EI benefits for a week, it will change the anticipated end date of entitlement. For example:

An EI claim is started on January 9, 2011. As the benefit period normally last 52 weeks, the EI claim will end January 7, 2012. The claimant was entitled to 33 weeks of EI benefits.

  • The claimants two week waiting period is from January 9 to 22, 2011, meaning an anticipated end week of September 3, 2011.
  • The claimant declared earning for six weeks in June and July and was not eligible for EI benefits for those weeks. The new anticipated end week is October 8, 2011.
  • The claimant works again in September and October for eight weeks. The new anticipated end week is December 17, 2011.

This re-confirmation of end date is necessary so that Financial Supports for basic living expenses are not paid in error to the Client.

Clients with a 900 series Social Insurance Number

A Social Insurance Number (SIN) beginning with the number "9" signals that the SIN holder is not a Canadian citizen or permanent resident and does not have the right to work in Canada without special permission.

Individuals who are not Canadian citizens or permanent residents (i.e. with 900 series SINs) may access the ESC Self-Serve Resource Area but there are limitations on access to other EPBC services to only those who meet specific criteria. For further information, please refer to the detailed policies and procedures on Clients with a 900 series SIN.

Clients in Receipt of EI Special Benefits (Sick, Maternity, Parental or Compassionate Care)

EI claimants who are in receipt of sick, maternity, parental or compassionate care benefits receive them for very specific reasons. They are ill and unable to work, have temporarily left employment because of the birth or adoption of a child or are staying at home to care for a newborn or adopted child. Persons in receipt of compassionate care benefits are providing psychological or emotional support, or arranging for care by a third party, or directly providing or participating in the care to a family member. This absence from the labour market or employment is for a predetermined period of time and the reason for unemployment is specific.

These EI claimants are not required to be actively looking for work as they have employment to return to when the special circumstances are over. EI claimants who are collecting Special Benefits can access some EPBC services; however it is unlikely that they would do so, as they have a job to return to. If a Client on Special Benefits asks about services through the EPBC, the Client should not be considered for Case Management or other services, unless he or she can meet the Program definition of "Unemployed." If the Client does not have a job to return to, he or she could access Case Management and services that provide LMDA funded Financial Supports (Training, Self-Employment or paid work experience through WS or JCP placements).

For an EI claimant in receipt of Special Benefits to be referred to a training, SE, JCP or WS service under the EPBC, the Client must request that the EI Part I claim be changed to a Regular Benefits claim, if possible. Examples where the claimant may not qualify to change to Regular Benefits include:

  • The claimant does not have the required number of hours to qualify for Regular Benefits;
  • The claimant does not have a sufficient number of weeks of entitlement weeks payable while on a claim; or
  • The claimant was disqualified prior to receiving the Special Benefits.

If the claimant does not qualify for Regular Benefits, he/she would still be an EI Client and eligible for the EPBC service. However, the circumstances supporting the Special Benefits would no longer apply; therefore the Client would no longer qualify for EI Part I benefits and would require Financial Supports to assist with living expenses and other eligible costs if needed for Program participation.

Clients with Existing EI Overpayments

If a Client has an EI overpayment when applying for Financial Supports, the Client should request a deferral of that overpayment recovery until after the end of the EPBC service, if a deferral is not possible the client should arrange a repayment schedule. Financial Supports should not be increased to pay the EI overpayment as this is considered an inappropriate use of public funds; however, if the Case Manager has determined the Clients Action Plan is appropriate, the existence of an overpayment, whether or not a repayment plan is in place, should not affect the referral decision.

Clients in Receipt of Severance Pay

Severance pay is a form of recognition for years of service and is paid to compensate for the loss of employment. It is usually paid only when the employer-employee relationship completely terminates. Severance pay is earnings paid or payable by reason of a lay-off or separation. For purposes of EI, it is allocated at normal weekly earnings from the date of lay-off or separation depending on which event gave rise to the severance monies being paid.

Consequently, a Client is not eligible to receive EI Part I benefits until the severance amount is fully allocated against any EI benefits the Client would otherwise have received. However, a Client is eligible for participation in an EPBC service that requires a Section 25 Referral if that person has established an EI Part I benefit period, even though the Client is not being paid EI Part I benefits.

In rare cases, when a Client has been assessed as eligible for an EPBC service (training, SE, JCP), Financial Supports for living expenses may be provided to Clients whose severance pay or buy-out package is being allocated in accordance with Part I of the EI Act.

This could be appropriate in situations where the Client does not have enough money to cover living expenses before his/her Part I benefits become payable (e.g. where the Client will be using most of the severance/buy-out money to roll into a Registered Savings Plan or to invest in a business he/she is setting up under SE Services). However, if sufficient money is still available to the Client to meet his/her needs during the period of allocated earnings, Financial Supports should not be provided since, other than JCP allowances which provide remuneration for work on a project, all Financial Supports are provided only when a Client is assessed as needing public funding.

Decisions should be made on a case-by-case basis based upon the Clients financial needs assessment and unique circumstances.

ExceptionJCP participants: As JCP participants are receiving Financial Supports for living expenses as remuneration for the work they are doing on the JCP project, there is a need to maintain parity with other JCP participants on the same project. Therefore a JCP participant who is in receipt of severance pay which affects EI Part I benefits must receive Financial Supports for the JCP project work.

Work Sharing

Work-Sharing is an adjustment program administered through the EI Part I by Service Canada. Work-Sharing is designed to help employers and employees avoid temporary layoffs when there is a reduction in the normal level of business activity that is beyond the control of the employer. Under a Work-Sharing Agreement, the employer shortens the work week and pays reduced wages accordingly. For the hours, days, or shifts not worked, Service Canada arranges for EI eligible workers to draw benefits, to help compensate for the lower wages they receive from the employer.

The program helps employers retain skilled employees and avoid the costly process of recruiting and training new employees when business returns to normal levels. It also helps employees maintain their skills and jobs by supplementing their wages with EI benefits for the days they are not working.

EI Clients on Work-Sharing Agreements are receiving EI benefits under special circumstances. EI Clients on Work-Sharing are not "Unemployed" in the true sense of the word. They are still attached to their employer and are obligated to work a percentage of their time with the employer; the intent of the program is for the employee to return to their former work arrangement.

Work-Sharing Clients are not required to be actively looking for work as they have employment to return to when the special circumstances are over. EI Clients on a Work-Sharing arrangement can access some EPBC services; however it is unlikely that they would do so. However, if a Client on an EI Work-Sharing claim asks about services through the EPBC, the Client should not be considered for Case Management or other services, as he or she does not met the Program definition of "Unemployed."

Interstate Claims

There is an agreement between Canada and the United States regarding the collection of Unemployment Benefits (known in Canada as EI benefits) of the other country, if eligible. While this agreement allows the collection of the other countrys benefits, it does not allow for participation in employment programs.

A Canadian citizen or permanent resident living in Canada and collecting U.S. Employment Insurance under the Reciprocal Agreement is not eligible for any employment program service based on a US claim.

Disqualified / Disentitled Clients

A Client who has been disqualified or disentitled from receiving EI Part I benefits is eligible for participation in an EPBC service that requires EI Client status since the Client only has to establish a benefit period and not actually be in receipt of EI benefits (except in the case of maternity or parental benefits for 60-month Reachback Clients).

As part of the determination of Client eligibility for participation in an EPBC service that requires the Client to have EI Client status, the circumstances leading up to the disentitlement or disqualification should be explored with the Client. However, if an EPBC Case Manager has determined that the Clients Action Plan is appropriate, the existence of a disqualification or disentitlement should not affect the referral decision. (See also Ministry Policy on Training Services).

Paying Financial Supports when Part I is not payable

Financial Supports for living expenses during the period for which EI Part I benefits are not payable (i.e.: waiting period, allocation of earnings) should only be considered and approved in cases of Financial Burden. Caution must be taken to ensure that the definition of "Financial Burden" does not become the norm, taking on a broader interpretation than is intended.

In a situation where an individual establishes a new EI Part I benefit period in the middle of a period of approved Financial Supports, the two-week waiting period must still be served. Caution must be exercised not to circumvent the intent of the EI waiting period by paying Financial Supports to assist with living expenses during this period. It is, however, possible to continue to provide any other Financial Supports for Clients needed for participation that are included in the Clients Financial Agreement with the EPBC Service Provider.

Apprentices and two-week EI waiting Period

EI Regulations state that registered Apprentices only need serve one two-week waiting period for their whole apprenticeship program. The single waiting period would be served at the beginning of the first classroom portion of the apprenticeship. Once a waiting period is served for reasons related to apprenticeship, the waiting period is considered served regardless of the number of benefit periods required as long as the classroom instruction is related to the same apprenticeship. If a claimant abandons one apprenticeship for another, a new waiting period would have to be served.

Although an Apprentice only has to serve one two-week waiting period for the Apprenticeship Training, the waiting period must be served while the Apprentice is in the classroom.

Authorization to Quit Employment

Under the EI Act, a person is disqualified from receiving benefits if they voluntarily leave (quit) their employment without "just cause". In addition, they are not able to use the insurable hours from the employment to qualify for benefits when making a subsequent claim (Section 30 of the EI Act).

However, persons who leave their employment in order to take a course or program of training or to participate in an employment activity which has been approved by the Commission or the designated authority are considered to have just cause for doing so. In BC, an "Authorization to Quit" can only be made by a Ministry (ELMSD) official to whom this authority has been delegated.

Under the EPBC, an Authorization to Quit may be given in very exceptional circumstances to individuals who request services beyond the Self-Serve resources and are employed. This would enable them to access Supplemental Services (Training, paid work experience through WS or JCP placements, and SelfEmployment Services).

Exceptional circumstances are situations where Clients:

  • Are in receipt of a notice of imminent lay-off (in writing); or
  • Must leave their current occupation due to medical reasons (must provide written documentation from their doctor); and/or
  • Are in one of the following situations and meet the conditions specified below:
  • Are working less than 20 hours per week and are actively seeking full-time work; or
  • Have a lack of marketable skills and are in a job which is insecure and low-paying (i.e. does not provide sufficient income to enable the Client to support him or herself); or
  • Are underemployed Immigrants in insecure employment, with foreign credentials and marketable skills who require an upgrade to their qualifications.

Conditions:

  • Clients have been looking for, and are unable to find, other more suitable employment without quitting their current job;
  • Clients need skills training to obtain Sustainable Employment;
  • Clients are likely to be eligible for EI; and
  • Quitting their job would not adversely affect the employer.

These situations may warrant consideration for an Authorization to Quit Employment by the Ministry.

In such situations, the EPBC Service Provider may refer the Client for a Formal Needs Assessment and if the Client is determined as needing a Supplemental Service from the Program (Training, Paid Work Experience, SE) and the Client meets the Authorization to Quit conditions, the EPBC Service Provider must contact the Ministry immediately for an opinion as to the Clients potential eligibility for an Authorization to Quit.

As the Ministry cannot make a determination as to eligibility for EI Part I benefits, the Client must understand the risks and conditions of an Authorization to Quit and cannot quit his or her job without formally applying for an Authorization to Quit Employment and receiving an "Authorization to Quit" letter from the Ministry.

Note: EPBC Case Managed Clients who are on multi-year or multi- semester training courses are encouraged to find employment during long breaks (more than three weeks). If a multi-year/semester Client on a long break has to quit employment to return to school to complete their program, this can be supported. Case Managers should discuss long breaks in employment with their Clients and document on the EPBC Client Case File the possibility that the Ministry will need to provide an Authorization to Quit Employment for the long break.

In cases where the person has requested an Authorization to Quit Employment in order to take a course or program of training or to participate in an EPBC service under SE Services or JCP, the authorization should only be made if the person will also be provided with a Section 25 Referral by the Ministry to the course or employment service. The date of leaving employment should be the latest feasible date prior to starting the course or other employment activity.

Clients who have not acquired enough hours to establish an EI claim should still be encouraged to request an Authorization to Quit in order to "protect" the hours that they have accumulated for future use. Requests to backdate an Authorization to Quit will not be considered.

Service Canada places the onus on the claimant to provide them with an Authorization to Quit form or letter to support their claim for EI benefits.

    • Using the established system check, at least once a week, check the Clients EI Status on LMDA Access and follow up with EPBC Service Provider within four weeks if the claim is not finalized;
    • If needed, contact Service Canada through the established process, using the centralized LMDA Mailbox to follow up; and
    • If needed, contact the EPBC Case Manager to follow up with the Client.
  • If a claim status is "ActiveSpecial Benefits" (Parental/ Maternity/Sick /Compassionate Care):
    • Confirm with the Client and Case Manager that the Client has applied to Service Canada for the EI claim to be changed to Regular Benefits. The Section 25 Referral cannot be input until the claim is switched to "ActiveRegular".
  • Once the Section 25 entry is entered and accepted in LMDA Access, sign the authorization referral and agreement form on behalf of the Ministry, and send it back to the EPBC Case Manage through ICM.

22

Consider Authorization to Quit Employment (ATQ) circumstances
  • Determine whether, based on the following ATQ criteria, it is reasonable and appropriate in specific Client situations to consider supporting an ATQ and referral to a Formal Needs Assessment (FNA):
    • The goal of the EPBC is to assist Unemployed British Columbians prepare for, find and maintain Employment. If an individual is not Unemployed but needs EPBC services or Financial Supports to achieve Sustainable Labour Market Attachment, a request for an ATQ may be considered under specific circumstances. An ATQ to participate in an employment service will only be provided in exceptional situations. For example if an individual:
    • Is in receipt of a notice of imminent lay-off (in writing); or
    • Must leave their current occupation due to medical reasons (must provide written documentation from their doctor); and/or
    • Is in one of the following situations and meets the conditions specified below:
    • Working less than 20 hours per week and actively seeking full-time work; and/or
    • Has a lack of marketable skills and is in a job which is insecure and low-paying (does not provide sufficient income to enable the Client to support him/herself); and/or
    • Is an underemployed Immigrant in insecure employment, with foreign credentials, requiring skills upgrading to meet qualification requirements in Canada.
    • If an individual meets any of the criteria above, and is a Client with a disability, he or she should be supported in exploring whether EPBC services or disability-related supports might enable job retention. This should be thoroughly explored with the Client and employer, prior to being considered for an ATQ.
    • ATQ Conditions:
    • Individual has been looking for, and is unable to find, other more suitable employment without quitting his/her current job;
    • Individual needs skills training to obtain Sustainable Employment; and
    • Individual is likely to be eligible for EI Part I benefits.
    • An ATQ should only be approved if the authorized Ministry (ELMSD) official would also authorize a Section 25 Referral for the Client to participate in a specific service.
 V

23

Review specific Client situation for an ATQ request
  • For any individual who does not meet the Program definition of Unemployed, and is requesting a FNA for EPBC services, ensure that the individual understands the purpose and intent of EPBC services.
  • Collect Employment Readiness information from the Client and complete a Multi-Dimensional Needs Assessment (MDNA) to determine the Clients Employment Readiness and confirm the need for EPBC services and supports to achieve Sustainable Labour Market Attachment.
  • During the assessment, discuss the Clients situation with him/her. Consider the individuals situation and determine if the Client meets the exceptional circumstance criteria. Key factors to consider include:
    • Whether the Client already possesses the skills and ability to find other suitable employment without EPBC services, and if so, should continue to seek employment without quitting the his/her current job;
    • An assessment and confirmation of financial need;
    • Whether the Client has a reasonable, realistic and achievable Employment Goal;
    • The viability of the individuals current employment situation, based on their individual needs and circumstances; and
    • The impact of the Client quitting on the employer. While decisions related to ATQ are primarily based on Client needs, employer impacts are an important consideration as employers also pay into the EI account and their businesses could be negatively impacted by a Client quitting employment. Care must be taken to consider both the Client and employer in determining the appropriateness of an ATQ.
V

24

Gather ATQ request information

  • Gather required information from the Client to support an ATQ request, including the following information:
    • Current employment situation including income level, hours and type of work and reasons the Client is requesting to quit employment;
    • Information on why training is needed and how it will improve the individuals employment situation;
    • Other options explored, including efforts to job search without leaving employment; and
    • Training program information.
 V

25

Determine support for an ATQ application to the Ministry

  • Decide whether to support the Clients request for an ATQ approval. A request must be completed and submitted to the Ministry, along with all required documentation, prior to the Client leaving employment.
  • If the ATQ request is not supported by the Case Manager, explain the reason to the Client and document the rationale in the EPBC Client Case File in ICM. In this situation, the Client should be encouraged to access the WorkBC Employment Services Centre (ESC) Self-Serve Resource Area for job search, if appropriate.
  • If the ATQ is supported by the Case Manager, assist the Client as needed to complete the ATQ application, scan and attach it to an ICM Service Request, and submit the Clients application to the Ministry (ELMSD).
 V

26

Approve / deny ATQ applications

  • Review and assess the Clients ATQ application.
  • Request other information or clarification from the EPBC Case Manager if necessary.
  • Communicate all decisions to the Case Manager, including the rationale.
  • In the EPBC Client Case File, document the decision rationale which should include:
    • If denied: Reasons for denial of the ATQ application; or
    • If approved: Information on why training is needed and how training will improve the individuals employment situation; other options explored and why leaving employment is necessary, and Training program information.

V 

27

Inform Client of ATQ decision

  • Inform the Client of the decision to support the ATQ, or the reasons for denial.
  • Record the decision on the "Authorization to Quit form". Ensure three copies are made, and distribute them as follows:
    • One copy to the Clients EPBC Client Case File; and
    • Two copies to the individual; one for the Clients records, and one to submit with his/her EI application.
 V

28

Work until EPBC training start date

  • For Clients approved to quit their employment, continue to work until as close to the start date of training as possible. Normally an ATQ is not approved more than 2 weeks prior to the training start date.
    • To meet and maintain EI eligibility, EI claimants must be available and able to work until the start date of approved training. EI claimants/recipients should indicate on their EI reporting card(s) that they are available and able to work, including during the interval between leaving employment and commencing training.
  • Ensure that one copy of the signed "Authorization to Quit" form is provided to Service Canada together with the application for EI Part I benefits. Clients should be aware of the risk to their EI approval should they not include the form.
 V

29

Communicate ATQ approval to Service Canada (exceptional situations)

  • There are no systems to communicate an ATQ directly between the provincial and federal government. In rare instances where there is concern that a Client may not be able to provide the documentation to EI in a timely manner, the communication process allows for this information to be shared to ensure that the Clients EI is not adversely impacted.
